Life History Strategy Space

Every species solves the same equation differently: live fast, reproduce often, die young — or live slow, reproduce rarely, invest heavily. This is the r/K continuum, one of ecology's deepest organizing ideas.

Why does an albatross live 60 years and lay one egg every two years, while a sparrow lives 3 years and lays a dozen?

X axis: Lifespan (years, log scale). Each point is a species.

r-strategists (bottom-left): short-lived, many offspring — House Sparrow, Common Cuckoo, Zebra Finch.

K-strategists (top-right): long-lived, few offspring — Albatrosses, Kakapo, large raptors.

Diet & Anatomy: Form Follows Function

Beak shape is rarely arbitrary — it's a direct readout of diet. Plot beak length against curvature, colored by diet, and watch unrelated species cluster by what they eat, not who they're related to.

Why do hummingbirds, sunbirds, and lorikeets — separated by 60+ million years of evolution — all end up with similar beaks?

Geographic Mysteries

Where a species lives is shaped by climate, competition, and history as much as by what it could theoretically tolerate. Filter by region and altitude to find patterns — and absences.

Why are there no hummingbirds outside the Americas? Why no woodpeckers in Australia? Geography remembers continental drift.

Conservation: The Web of Dependencies

IUCN status is a snapshot, not a sentence — but the threats behind each status tell a deeper story. Many declines trace back to a handful of root causes, repeated across unrelated species worldwide.

If one habitat disappears, how many separate evolutionary stories disappear with it?

Side by Side

Pick two or three species — related or wildly different — and compare them trait by trait. Sometimes the most interesting comparisons are between species that share nothing but a single solved problem.

Try: Bee Hummingbird vs Wandering Albatross. Or: Peregrine Falcon vs Andean Condor — both raptors, opposite strategies entirely.
